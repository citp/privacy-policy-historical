  4. Non-Personally-Identifiable Information Collected Automatically Which Could Theoretically be Linked With Other Databases in the Future

    Internet advertising networks such as DoubleClick.net (which is used on this web site) are currently the focus of regulatory and legislative scrutiny concerning the breadth and ultimate use of information collected from websites like iFriends. When you navigate through iFriends, DoubleClick may set cookies during your viewing of certain pages. iFriends utilizes the data which DoubleClick has collected in this fashion to determine how far you have progressed into the iFriends site. (This information is of great value to iFriends as it can be used to determine which advertisements have the greatest likelihood of ultimately leading you to the iFriends Membership Signup page or to other iFriends pages where products or services are sold).

    As you navigate through iFriends, DoubleClick has the opportunity to record the "referring URL" of the page you viewed while the cookie was written. For example, a referring URL such as http://apps7.ifriends.net/~wsapi/ifbrowse.dll?room=2&type=L&filter=n contains certain information intended for, and read by, iFriends' web servers, and which is generally understood only by iFriends' programmers and technical staff. For example, "room=2&type=L&filter=n" indicates that the user is viewing a series of videochat rooms which are in the "Psychics/Advice" category (room=2), are displayed in the "list" format (type=L) and which are "filtered" in such a way as no adult-oriented content should appear (filter=n).

    Although iFriends does not provide DoubleClick or any other party with a reference or index that can be used to translate the meaning of these codes, DoubleClick or other third parties have the technical and theoretical capability to build a database dictionary, based upon manual, human labor by substantial numbers of DoubleClick staff who, through an observational trial-and-error scheme, may ultimately be able to independently compile an accurate index of what each iFriends room number relates to, as well as dozens of other variables that at one time or another are visible in the iFriends referral URL. Were such a dictionary ultimately produced, and were it maintained in such a way that every time iFriends altered its internal linking syntax, a human at DoubleClick observed the change and performed a corresponding update in DoubleClick's own dictionary, the information could potentially be associated with useful information about you that DoubleClick has collected from other sources, and which ultimately be added and stored to an online "profile" about you. (To continue the example, such an online profile might record your preference for and interest in psychics/astrology).

    However, since there are thousands of commercial web services using DoubleClick products and services in a manner similar to iFriends, the already complex task of organizing and maintaining "decoding" dictionary databases and reference works related to each website is multipled in complexity by the number of different websites that could conceivably be tracked. Absent such a labor-intensive undertaking, the referral URL information exposed to DoubleClick as you traverse iFriends is unintelligible gibberish. Nevetheless, the computer and internet industries are known to progress at extremely rapid rates, and there is no guarantee that the necessary technology and manpower will not exist in the future to prevent such online "profiles" from ever being compiled.

    iFriends' contract with DoubleClick gives iFriends' ultimate ownership of all collected data, and DoubleClick is not entitled to use it other than for general reporting of aggregate information. Should regulations and laws evolve which impose limitations and restrictions on iFriends' collection and use of such data (beyond the self-imposed limitations iFriends already imposes upon itself, set forth in this privacy policy), iFriends will be pleased to abide by such laws/regulations.

  5. Information That May Be Placed Automatically On Your Computer's Hard Drive

    When you view one of our websites, we, or certain third parties such as DoubleClick.net, may store some information on your computer. This information will be in the form of a "Cookie" or similar file and will help us in many ways. For example, cookies allow us to tailor web pages to better match your interests and preferences. iFriends also utilizes services provided by certain third parties such as DoubleClick.net which enable iFriends to determine which advertisements are most likely to lead to a certain event or outcome, such as a purchase transaction. Such cookies also can be used to present advertisements to you which you may have not seen before or to present advertisements to you which your browser is known to be capable of displaying, such as "rich media" advertisements (advertisements with sound, video, etc). With most Internet Browsers, you can erase Cookies from your computer hard drive, block all Cookies, or receive a warning before a Cookie is stored. Please refer to your Browser instructions or help screen to learn more about these functions.

    1. How to erase cookies from your hard drive
      Netscape Navigator/Communciator
      Windows users: Make sure that Netscape is not running. Using the "My Computer" or "Windows Explorer", browse to "Program Files", then "Netscape", then "Users", then your current user profile folder (often "default"). Within this folder, locate the file called "cookies" or "cookies.txt". Right-click on this file and select "Delete".

      Mac users: Make sure that Netscape is not running. Go to your Mac System Folder, open Preferences, and open "Netscape Users". Locate the "Magic Cookie" file, drag it to the Trash, then empty the trash. Once you restart Navigator or Communicator, your cookies will be gone.

      Unix users: Make sure that Netscape is not running. Simply delete the "cookies" file in your ".netscape" directory.

      Microsoft Internet Explorer
      Windows users: Make sure that Internet Explorer is not running. Using the "My Computer" or "Windows Explorer", browse to the Windows folder. If you are using Windows 3.1 or Windows 95/98 without user profiles enabled, double-click the "Cookies" folder. If you are using Windows 95 with user profiles enabled or Windows NT, double-click the "Profiles" folder, double-click the folder with the same name as the name you use to log on to Windows, then double-click the Cookies folder. Locate the username@site.txt file (username is the name you use to log on to Windows and site is the name of the site whose cookie you wish to delete). Click the cookie file, then click Delete on the File menu. If you wish to delete ALL cookies, just delete all the file that follow that are named username@site.txt.

      Mac users: The instructions for deleting cookies depend on which version of MSIE you are using. For MSIE 2.x, simply delete the System Folder:Preferences:Explorer:Cookies.txt file. For MSIE 3.0, Delte the System Folder:Preferences:Internet Preferences file (however, this may affect other Internet-based applications, so do not delete this file unless you have written down all of the settings your others applications may need). For MSIE 3.01a, on the Edit menu, click Preferences, then, under "Receiving Files" click Cookies, then the cookies you wish to delete, click delete, and click OK. For MSIE 4.x, on the Edit menu, click Preferences, then cookies, click the cookies you wish to delete, click delete, then OK.

      Unix users: Go to your home directory, then change to the .microsoft directory within. Change to the Cookies directory inside. Delete all .txt files within this directory (username@site.txt).


    2. How to prevent iFriends or other parties from writing cookies to your hard drive
      Netscape Navigator/Communicator
      From within the Netscape application, click the "Edit" pull-down menu and click "Preferences...". When the Preferences window appears, click "Advanced". Click "Disable Cookies", then click OK. This will prevent your browser from accepting any site's cookies.

      Microsoft Inernet Explorer
      From within the Internet Explorer application, click the "Tools" pull-down menu and click "Internet Options...". When the Internet Options window appears, click the "Security" tab. Then, click the "Custom Level...". Scroll down the Settings options until you get to the Cookies section. Set both "Allow..." options under cookies to "Disable". Click "OK", and "OK" again. This will prevent your browser from accepting any site's cookies.
